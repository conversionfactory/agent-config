# Section Patterns

Common section types for marketing websites and how to approach each one. These are starting points — the layout should always be informed by the copy and what you're trying to get someone to feel.

## Hero

The first thing anyone sees. Its job is to communicate the core value and earn the scroll.

- Headline is the star — give it room and emphasis
- Subheadline expands, doesn't repeat
- One primary CTA, optionally one secondary
- Hero animations can hold attention but don't need to loop forever
- Background treatments (gradients, textures, illustrations) set the mood for the entire page

## Social Proof Bar

Builds credibility early. Usually logos, stats, or a quote.

- Logo marquees: evaluate whether they need to keep scrolling or can be static
- Keep it tight — this section earns trust, it doesn't need to be a destination
- Can sit between hero and first content section as a bridge

## Problem / Pain

Shows the visitor you understand their situation before you pitch.

- Use the customer's language, not the company's
- Can be structured as questions, scenarios, or a narrative
- Don't linger — acknowledge the pain, then move to the solution

## Solution / Benefits

Connects features to outcomes. Not "what it does" but "what that means for you."

- 3-5 benefits is the sweet spot
- Each benefit gets one clear statement and supporting detail
- Consider alternating layout (left-right-left) for visual rhythm
- Icons or illustrations should support, not decorate

## How It Works

Reduces perceived complexity. Makes the product feel approachable.

- 3-4 steps maximum
- Each step: short title + one sentence
- Numbered or sequential layout implies simplicity
- A visual showing the product in action can replace or supplement the steps

## Features

Detailed capability showcase. Can be one section or multiple.

- Individual feature sections: alternate image/text layout for rhythm
- Feature grids: useful for many small features, but avoid making them all look identical
- Each feature connects to a benefit — "what" + "so what"
- Don't reuse the same icon for different features

## Testimonials

Social proof through customer voices.

- Named, specific quotes > anonymous praise
- Photo + name + title + company for credibility
- Consider one featured testimonial (large, prominent) vs. a grid of shorter ones
- Testimonial sections break up the pitch — they're breathing room for the reader

## Pricing

Helps visitors choose the right plan.

- Make the recommended plan visually obvious
- Address "which is right for me?" anxiety
- Feature comparison tables for 3+ plans
- CTA on every plan, not just the featured one

## CTA / Conversion

The closing argument. Appears near the bottom, sometimes mid-page too.

- Recap the core value proposition
- Risk reversal (free trial, money-back guarantee, no credit card)
- High visual contrast with surrounding sections — this earns a dark/light flip
- Single, clear action

## FAQ

Handles objections the rest of the page didn't address.

- Real questions, not marketing fluff disguised as questions
- Keep answers concise — link out for depth
- Accordion or open layout depending on volume

## Footer

Navigation utility, not a design showcase.

- Links are secondary by default, primary on hover
- Not every link needs emphasis — hierarchy matters here too
- Legal, social links, newsletter signup if applicable
- Keep it clean and scannable

---

## Section Rhythm Principles

- Never use the same layout for consecutive sections
- Evaluate each section in context of what's above and below it
- A page should have a pace: dense sections followed by spacious ones, detailed sections followed by simple ones
- Dark/light transitions (when used) should mark narrative shifts, not just alternate for variety
- Consolidate where possible — five thin sections are worse than three substantial ones
